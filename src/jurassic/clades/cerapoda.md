Diagnosed by a pronounced diastema—a distinct, toothless gap between the cropping beak at the very front of the mouth and the grinding cheek teeth in the back. This specialized gap functioned exactly like it does in modern horses, allowing them to rapidly crop fresh vegetation with their beaks while simultaneously using their long tongues to shift food to the back for continuous chewing.
